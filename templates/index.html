The Back Story

The opioid drug problem has reached crisis levels in the United States. Over 50,000 Americans died of a drug overdose in 2015, of which 63 percent (33,091) reportedly involved opioids. The Center for Disease Control (CDC) reports that on average, 115 Americans die every day from an opioid overdose. The problem is worsening at an alarming pace, with opioid-involved overdose deaths doubling in the past ten years and quadrupling in the past sixteen.

The Council of Economic Advisers to the White House (CEA) estimates that in 2015, the economic cost of the opioid crisis was $504.0 billion*, or 2.8 percent of GDP that year. This is over six times larger than the most recently estimated economic cost of the epidemic.

*Estimate is based on the economic cost of these deaths using conventional economic estimates for valuing life routinely used by U.S. Federal agencies.

The council report among other factors prompted the Trump Administration to declare the US Opioid Crisis as a national Public Health Emergency and sign the INTERDICT Act on January 10, 2018.

Our Purpose

Since 1999, the death rate due to drug overdoses in the United States has increased exponentially. In January 2018, the White House officially declared the Opioid Crisis a national emergency. We have depicted this rise in death rates due to drug overdoses over the last 20 years, filtering by gender and opioid type. In that time frame, we have also identified the states with the greatest and least death rate per year and overall. In developing this project, our purpose is to educate Americans with these visualizations. We hope that policy makers, activists, and concerned citizens can use these visualizations to develop solutions to tackle this issue.
